10.13

Chickens are rather aggressive birds that tend to establish dominance over other chickens by pecking them—hence the term “pecking order.” So for any two chick- ens in a farmyard, either the first pecks the second, or the second pecks the first, but not both. We say that chicken uuu virtually pecks chicken vvv if either:

  • Chicken uuu pecks chicken vvv, or
  • Chicken uuu pecks some other chicken www who in turn pecks chicken vvv.

A chicken that virtually pecks every other chicken is called a king chicken.

We can model this situation with a chicken digraph whose vertices are chickens, with an edge from chicken uuu to chicken vvv precisely when uuu pecks vvv. In the graph in Figure 10.11, three of the four chickens are kings. Chicken ccc is not a king in this example since it does not peck chicken bbb and it does not peck any chicken that pecks chicken bbb. Chicken aaa is a king since it pecks chicken ddd , who in turn pecks chickens bbb and ccc.

In general, a tournament digraph is a digraph with exactly one edge between each pair of distinct vertices.

  • a) State the definition of a graph isomorphism from a graph GGG to a graph HHH.

    GGG and HHH are isomorphic if there is a bijection from the vertex set of GGG to the vertex set of HHH that preserves adjacency and nonadjacency.

  • b) Use that definition to prove that the relation R={(G,H):GR = \{(G, H): GR={(G,H):G is isomorphic to HHH }\}} is an equivalence relation on the set of undirected graphs.

    A={Gset:GsetA = \{G_{set} : G_{set}A={Gset​:Gset​ set of undirected graphs }\}}

    • Reflexive: ∀G∈A\forall G \in A∀G∈A, (G,G)∈R(G, G) \in R(G,G)∈R

      If graph GGG is an undirected graph, GGG would map each vertex to itself in another GGG, so GGG is isomorphic to GGG, so reflexive. ✓\checkmark✓

    • Symmetric: ∀G,H∈A\forall G, H \in A∀G,H∈A, (G,H)∈R→(H,G)∈R(G, H) \in R \rightarrow (H, G) \in R(G,H)∈R→(H,G)∈R

      If graph GGG and HHH are undirected graphs and (GGG, HHH) is isomorphic, then (HHH, GGG) would also be isomorphic via an inverse(or reverse) of the function that mapped GGG to HHH. So symmetric. ✓\checkmark✓

    • Transitive: ∀G,H,I∈A\forall G, H, I \in A∀G,H,I∈A (G,H)∈R∧(H,I)∈R→(G,I)∈R(G, H) \in R \wedge (H, I) \in R \rightarrow (G, I) \in R(G,H)∈R∧(H,I)∈R→(G,I)∈R

      If GGG, HHH and III are undirected graphs and (GGG, HHH) and (HHH, III) are isomorphic, then (GGG, III) are isomorphic because of bijectivity and the preserved adjacencies.

      In a different scenario where (GGG, HHH) is only bijective and (HHH, III) is only bijective, and (GGG, III) is bijective. This doesn't imply (GGG, III) is isomorphic, because a bijection only requires a one to one mapping of vertices and that every vertex in the other graph is mapped. These alone doesn't meet the extra requirements for an isomorphism.
